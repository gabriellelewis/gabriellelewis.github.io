The LGBTQ Community

and the Pandemic

Here's how it affected their lives.

By Gabrielle Lewis


The COVID-19 pandemic had immediate effects on everyone in the United States, from throwing many into social isolation to taking away valuable sources of income and people’s lives — and the LGBTQ population is no exception.

Over the past year during the pandemic, many studies have come out detailing the obstacles LGBTQ Americans face which would make the pandemic disproportionately affect them.

The Human Rights Campaign released an issue brief last year explaining many of the unique challenges within the LGBTQ community. Since LGBTQ Americans work in industries highly affected by the pandemic, about 5 million jobs were more likely to face consequences. The brief also included overviews of how LGBTQ Americans are more likely to live in poverty and have respiratory issues, as well as how LGBTQ youth are more likely to be homeless.

The challenges coming with this pandemic haven't just disproportionately affected the livelihoods within the community — they also exacerbated mental health issues by adding other stressors on top of LGBTQ-related ones.

How the pandemic hurt LGBTQ mental health


Before the pandemic, LGBTQ individuals already faced higher risks of mental health issues. LGBTQ youth are more likely than non-LGBTQ youth to struggle with their mental health, and LGBTQ adults are also more likely to have mental health or substance use issues, according to an issue brief from the HRC. These higher rates of mental health issues can be attributed to the stigma and discrimination surrounding the LGBTQ community.

On top of these preexisting risks, the pandemic added a plethora of stressors, such as social isolation, unemployment and the loss of loved ones. A national survey of LGBTQ college students during the pandemic found large numbers of students struggling with substance abuse, social isolation and psychological distress, as well as a lack of emotional support during the pandemic.

Although the pandemic has worsened mental health for LGBTQ people, it may not mean an increase in seeking out mental health services. At the University of Maryland’s counseling center, the number of LGBTQ students seeking out mental health services and mental health support systems, such as the center’s workshops, has dropped from the 2019-20 academic year to this current academic year.

But even though the pandemic has worsened mental health for LGBTQ people, it doesn’t mean there aren’t solutions.

The survey led by Salerno illustrated ways university leadership could help its LGBTQ student population. One recommendation was to collect data on students’ sexual orientation and gender identity to identify and address LGBTQ students’ needs. Another was to provide mental health services, such as chat or text-based services, which preserve LGBTQ students’ privacy and safety if they have not come out to their families.
